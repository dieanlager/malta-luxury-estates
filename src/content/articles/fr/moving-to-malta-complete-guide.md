# Moving to Malta in 2026 – The Complete Expat Guide

Malta is an English-speaking EU member state with a Mediterranean climate, low crime, excellent connectivity to Europe, and one of the EU's most favourable tax regimes for new residents. Around 12,000 new residents register annually. The practical challenges are: high property costs in prime areas, traffic congestion, and a healthcare system that works well privately but has public waiting lists.

## Why People Move to Malta

Malta consistently ranks among Europe's top destinations for relocation. The core appeal is a combination that is rare to find anywhere: English as an official language, EU membership and freedom of movement (for EU citizens), warm Mediterranean climate (300+ days of sunshine), low crime, and a tax regime that rewards new residents.

The typical profile of someone moving to Malta in 2026:

- **iGaming and tech professionals** recruited by the 300+ licensed operators based in Malta
- **Remote workers and digital nomads** using Malta's Nomad Residence Permit
- **Retirees from the UK** post-Brexit, seeking EU access and warm winters
- **HNWIs** using the Global Residence Programme or MPRP for tax efficiency
- **EU citizens** exercising free movement for lifestyle, business, or tax reasons
- **Students** (30,000+ annual English-language students, though most are short-stay)

## Before You Move: Key Decisions

### EU vs Non-EU: What You Need to Move to Malta

**EU/EEA/Swiss citizens:** You have the right to live and work in Malta without a visa. Register with the Identity Malta Agency (now Identità) within 3 months of arrival. You will receive an e-Residence card.

**British citizens (post-Brexit):** UK nationals no longer have automatic EU free movement rights. You need either a work permit sponsored by a Maltese employer, a self-employment licence, the Malta Digital Nomad Residence Permit (if working remotely for a non-Maltese company), or one of the investment programmes (MPRP, GRP). The process is more involved but very achievable.

**Non-EU nationals:** Various pathways exist depending on your circumstances:
- **Employment:** Employer applies for a Single Permit on your behalf
- **Self-employment:** Apply for a self-employment licence through Jobsplus
- **Digital nomads:** Malta Nomad Residence Permit (income threshold: €2,700/month net)
- **Investment/MPRP:** See our [full MPRP guide](/insights/malta-permanent-residency-mprp-property-guide)
- **Retirement (GRP):** Global Residence Programme – 15% flat tax on foreign income remitted to Malta, minimum tax of €15,000/year

## Where to Live: Malta vs Gozo and Choosing Your Area

### Malta Island

The main island has a population of ~530,000 concentrated in the Northern Harbour area (Sliema, St Julian's, Valletta, Gżira, Msida, Swieqi) and the suburban centre. This is where most expats live.

**Northern Harbour area (most expats):**

| Area | Character | Best for | Monthly rent (2-bed, furnished) |
|------|-----------|----------|--------------------------------|
| Sliema | Seafront, cosmopolitan, walkable | Established expats, retirees, families | €1,800–€2,400 |
| St Julian's | Vibrant, nightlife, Portomaso | Young professionals, iGaming workers | €2,000–€2,800 |
| Gżira | Central, value, Manoel Island views | Budget-conscious expats, young couples | €1,400–€1,900 |
| Swieqi | Quiet residential, near St Julian's | Families, professionals wanting peace | €1,500–€2,100 |
| Msida | University area, marina | Students, academics, young professionals | €1,200–€1,600 |
| Valletta | UNESCO historic, compact | Culture lovers, boutique buyers | €1,300–€2,200 |

**Other areas worth knowing:**

- **Mellieħa (North Malta):** Best beaches, family-oriented, slower pace. Long commute to Valletta/St Julian's (~35–45 min by car). Popular with UK retirees.
- **Naxxar / San Ġwann / Balzan:** Suburban Malta. More Maltese neighbours than expats, generally quieter. Good for families wanting space.
- **Marsaxlokk (South Malta):** Traditional fishing village, beautiful bay, very Maltese character. Growing expat presence. Long commute to north.

### Gozo

Malta's sister island has a population of ~33,000. Life is slower, greener, and significantly more affordable than Malta. The ferry from Gozo to Malta takes ~25 minutes, but including the drive to the ferry terminal, factor 50–70 minutes each way to reach St Julian's.

**Who chooses Gozo:**
- Writers, artists, retirees seeking quiet Mediterranean life
- Remote workers who rarely need to commute
- Those buying houses of character or farmhouses at significant value vs Malta
- Long-term Malta residents "escaping" the pace of the main island

**If the Gozo tunnel proceeds** (infrastructure decision pending), commuting will transform completely. Early buyers may benefit from the uplift.

## Cost of Living in Malta 2026

Malta is significantly cheaper than London, Amsterdam, or Zurich – but more expensive than Eastern Europe or Southern Italy. The main cost driver for expats is housing; everything else is broadly affordable.

**Note on electricity:** Malta's electricity is among the most expensive in the EU. Air conditioning is used 5–7 months of the year, pushing summer bills higher. Budget €200–€400/month for electricity in a typical apartment with A/C.

### What's Cheap in Malta

- Eating out at local restaurants (fenek – rabbit stew – for €12, pastizzi for €0.30)
- Alcohol (beer €2–€3 in a bar, wine from €3.50 in a supermarket)
- Domestic help and cleaning (€12–€18/hour, much lower than Western Europe)
- Fresh produce at local markets (very cheap vs UK/Germany/Netherlands)
- Petrol (broadly similar to EU average)
- No inheritance tax, no property tax, no wealth tax

### What's Expensive in Malta

- Rent in prime areas (not cheap by European standards)
- Private healthcare (necessary for quality of service)
- International school fees (€8,000–€25,000/year)
- Eating at premium restaurants and hotel bars
- New cars (imported, hence expensive – budget €5,000–€8,000 more than UK/Germany for equivalent car)

## Healthcare in Malta

### Public Healthcare

Malta has a universal public healthcare system (Mater Dei Hospital is the main facility). EU citizens with a valid EHIC/GHIC can access it. Standards of emergency care are good. Elective and specialist waiting lists are long by private-sector standards.

### Private Healthcare

The private healthcare sector is well-developed and widely used by expats. Main private hospitals: Boffa Hospital, St James Hospital, Capua Palace. Specialists are plentiful and generally trained in the UK, Ireland, or Italy.

**Health insurance cost (private, comprehensive):**
- Single adult, 30–40 years: €80–€150/month
- Family of four: €300–€500/month

Most employers provide health insurance as a standard benefit. If self-employed or on an investment visa, budget for private insurance from day one.

English-taught degrees, EU fees for EU students (much lower than UK post-Brexit). Popular with EU students and the base for a significant young professional community.

## Getting Around Malta

### The Reality About Traffic

Malta has one of the highest car ownership rates in the EU. Traffic in the Northern Harbour area (Sliema, St Julian's, Gżira) during morning and evening rush hours is genuinely bad. Budget 30–50 minutes for journeys that should take 10 minutes on a map.

**Most expats: own a car.** Public transport (bus network, run by Tallinja) covers the island but is slow and unreliable for commuting professionals.

**Alternative transport options:**
- **Tallinja buses:** Comprehensive network, €2 per journey (or €26/month pass). Good for occasional use; frustrating as a daily commute tool.
- **eCabs / Bolt:** Uber-equivalent, widely available. €5–€12 for most urban trips.
- **Electric scooters:** Growing in popularity in urban areas. WIND and other operators active.
- **Cycling:** Challenging due to hills, heat (May–October), and driver culture. Growing very slowly.

### Driving in Malta

**Left-hand traffic:** Malta drives on the left – a legacy of British rule. This is an advantage for UK drivers and a learning curve for everyone else.

**Driving licence conversion:** EU licence holders can drive immediately. Non-EU licence holders should convert to a Maltese licence (required within 12 months of becoming resident).

**Car purchase:** Used car market in Malta is strong. Expect to pay €1,500–€5,000 for a reliable used car. New cars are expensive due to import costs.

## Banking and Finance

### Opening a Bank Account in Malta

Opening a personal bank account in Malta requires:
- Valid passport
- Proof of Malta address (utility bill, lease agreement)
- Proof of income or source of funds

**Main banks:** Bank of Valletta (BOV), HSBC Malta, APS Bank, Lombard Bank, MeDirect (digital).

**Timeline:** BOV and HSBC can take 4–8 weeks due to compliance procedures. MeDirect is faster (digital process, typically 1–2 weeks).

**Practical tip:** Open your bank account before or immediately upon arrival. Many landlords require a Maltese bank account for rent direct debit. Some employers require it for payroll.

### Transferring Money Internationally

Using a bank for large international transfers is expensive (fees + poor exchange rates). Use:
- **Wise (formerly TransferWise):** Best for regular international transfers, transparent fees
- **Revolut:** Good for everyday multi-currency use and smaller transfers
- **OFX / TorFX:** Best for large single transfers (property purchase, investment)

## What to Know Before You Arrive

**The weather is genuinely hot in summer.** June–September temperatures regularly exceed 35°C and humidity is high. Air conditioning is not a luxury; it is essential. Ensure any property you rent or buy has modern, functioning A/C.

**Water is scarce.** Malta has no natural rivers. Water comes from desalination and groundwater. It is safe to drink (legally) but heavily chlorinated – most residents drink bottled water or use filters. Some properties draw from private wells; water quality varies.

**Malta is small.** At 316 km², it is the world's tenth smallest country. This means short distances (a bonus) but limited wilderness and open space. Claustrophobia is a real issue for some expats who are used to countryside access.

**The Maltese are direct.** Business and social culture is a blend of Mediterranean warmth and British directness. English is spoken everywhere. Maltese (Il-Malti) is the national language and is appreciated if you make any effort with it.

**The expat community is large and well-organised.** Facebook groups (Expats in Malta, British Expats Malta, Malta Digital Nomads), InterNations Malta, and various sports clubs mean that social integration is relatively easy, especially in St Julian's and Sliema.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Can British citizens still move to Malta after Brexit?
Yes, but you now need a visa or residency permit rather than relying on EU free movement. Options include a work permit (employer-sponsored), self-employment licence, Malta Nomad Residence Permit (for remote workers earning €2,700+/month), or investment-based pathways (MPRP, GRP). Malta actively welcomes British residents and the process, while more complex than pre-Brexit, is well-established.

### What is the minimum income for the Malta Nomad Residence Permit?
€2,700 net per month (€32,400/year). You must be employed by or contracted to a company outside Malta and working remotely. The permit is for one year, renewable. Application is made through Identity Malta (Identità).

### Is Malta safe to live in?
Malta consistently ranks among the safest countries in the EU. Violent crime is rare. The main safety concern for residents is road traffic – driving standards are variable and pedestrian infrastructure in some areas is poor.

### How long does it take to get a Maltese e-Residence card (EU citizens)?
Registration can be done at an Identità office. You receive a temporary registration certificate immediately; the physical e-Residence card typically takes 3–6 weeks to issue.

### Is English really spoken everywhere in Malta?
Yes. English is a co-official language and the primary language of business, law, healthcare, and education. You can live in Malta indefinitely without speaking Maltese. Almost all signage, legal documents, and government communications are in English.

### What is the tax situation for new residents in Malta?
Malta has a favourable non-dom regime for residents who are not Maltese domiciled. Foreign-source income not remitted to Malta is not taxed. For HNWIs, the Global Residence Programme offers a 15% flat tax on income remitted to Malta (minimum €15,000/year). Ordinary residents are taxed on worldwide income at progressive rates (15%–35%). Always take advice from a Maltese tax adviser before establishing residency.
